Brief intro

Handover of Hong Kong

Hong Kong was known for its vibrant and free media before the 1997 handover[1], and the transitional period was described as the time when the city’s press enjoyed an “unprecedented degree of freedom[2].”

In the first few years after 1997, the Beijing government granted a considerable degree of freedom by relying on “disciplining the Hong Kong press through self-censorship[3].”

Then chairperson of the Hong Kong Journalists Association, Mak Yin-ting, expressed concern[4] about the “Chinese model” of press freedom with which Hong Kong journalists were unfamiliar.

Article 23 Legislation Attempt

The Hong Kong government pushed for the legislation of Article 23 of the Basic Law, which prohibits acts such as treason, secession, sedition and subversion against the Central People's Government, as well as the theft of state secrets[1]. The equivalent national security law sparked concern in the media that it would severely restrict press freedom, particularly with regard to reporting on sensitive issues involving China or criticizing the authorities[2].

On July 1, 2003, an estimated 500,000 people protested against the bill[3]. This unprecedented level of public opposition demonstrated widespread anxiety about the bill's potential impact on freedom[4].

Faced with overwhelming public opposition and the collapse of support within the legislature, Hong Kong's first Chief Executive Tung Chee-hwa announced the withdrawal of the bill[5].
